01 — KEEP CUSTOMERS

RETENTION CAMPAIGNS

7 MIN READ

Most churn is predictable if you're watching the right signals. The problem is that the signals live in different systems — support tickets in one place, invoices in another, logins in a third, contact changes in a fourth. Nobody sees the pattern until the non-renewal email arrives.

A consolidated CRM pulls those signals into one view and acts on them automatically — which turns churn from a surprise into a managed workflow.

Two very different types of churn need two very different responses. Most programmes conflate them and under-deliver on both.

TWO FLAVOURS OF CHURN

VOLUNTARY VS INVOLUNTARY

Voluntary churn is typically 50–70% of total lost revenue — a customer decides to leave. Involuntary churn is 30–50% — a payment fails. The second is usually far cheaper to fix; most teams under-invest in it.

TYPE 01 VOLUNTARY CHURN Cause: customer actively decides to leave — price, product fit, usage drop, leadership change.

Fix: early-warning signals, proactive outreach, save offers, renewal-cycle pricing discipline.
TYPE 02 INVOLUNTARY CHURN Cause: payment fails — expired card, wrong billing contact, insufficient funds.

Fix: dunning sequences, smart retry logic, card-updater services, backup contact capture.
LEADING INDICATORS

SIGNALS THE CRM WATCHES

These signals are useless when they sit in four separate systems. They become powerful the moment they share a single customer record.

S1
SUPPORT SPIKE
JIRA / SUPPORT SYSTEM

Ticket volume jumps above the customer's rolling 90-day baseline. Frustration is often visible in support before it shows up in revenue. Threshold is per-customer, not a blanket rule — heavy users have higher baselines.

S2
USAGE DROP
PRODUCT ANALYTICS

Logins, module activity, or API calls declining week-on-week. The customer is already disengaging — the renewal conversation is just the event that makes it visible. Earliest fixable signal the system has.

S3
PAYMENT FRICTION
FINANCE / BILLING

Invoice paid late, payment method failing, partial payment, chargebacks. Sometimes just involuntary, sometimes a quiet way of expressing dissatisfaction. Either way, CRM opens a task rather than waiting for the next cycle.

S4
KEY CONTACT CHANGE
CONTACT DATA + LINKEDIN

Main buyer, champion, or decision-maker departs the customer. New person arrives with no history, no relationship, and no reason to keep using an incumbent system. The single most dangerous silent signal in B2B SaaS.

S5
ENGAGEMENT DECAY
COMMS DATA

Unanswered calls, unopened emails, missed quarterly reviews, declined meeting invites. The relationship is cooling. CRM turns that observation into a prioritised AM action rather than a feeling in someone's head.

THE RESPONSE LAYER

WHAT GETS TRIGGERED

Every signal maps to a pre-designed response. No ad-hoc decisions in the moment — the playbook is already written when the alert fires.

ACTION 01 AM ALERT & SCRIPT Account manager gets a prioritised alert with context — signal type, account history, recent interactions — and a suggested outreach script tailored to the trigger. Zero blank-page moments.
ACTION 02 AUTO-NUDGE EMAILS Low-risk signals (e.g. declining usage of one module) trigger automated, branded nudges: feature highlights, help-centre deep links, recorded training. Human-light, system-heavy.
ACTION 03 ESCALATION Serious signals (payment failure + usage drop + contact change) escalate to a senior AM or product team. Critical accounts never sit in an automation queue.
ACTION 04 EARLY RENEWAL TALK Renewal conversations opened 60–90 days early — while there's still time to fix problems rather than defend price. Turns renewal from a confrontation into a check-in.
WHEN SOMEONE SAYS "WE'RE LEAVING"

SAVE OFFERS & WIN-BACK

Every cancellation reason gets a different save offer. A blanket discount is lazy and expensive — it trains customers to threaten to leave for a cheaper rate. The CRM targets the offer to the actual reason.

  • "Too expensive" → downgrade path, flex-term, or annual-prepay discount — not a price drop on the current plan.
  • "Not using it" → usage audit, dedicated enablement, or pause-and-return offer. Fix the value problem, not the price.
  • "Moving to competitor" → feature gap analysis + escalation to product. Signal goes to the roadmap.
  • "Team changed" → introduction package, re-onboarding for new champion, free training credits.
  • "Company restructuring" → pause, downgrade, or flexible hold. Don't burn the bridge for the rebound.

Win-back sequences run automatically for 90 days post-cancellation — quarterly check-ins, product update emails, reactivation offers timed to likely re-evaluation moments. Many cancelled customers come back; the CRM makes that path frictionless.

INVOLUNTARY CHURN — THE QUIET KILLER

DUNNING & SMART RETRY

Failed payments quietly cost mature businesses 2–4% of annual revenue. Most of it is recoverable with a decent dunning sequence. This is the highest-ROI retention work a CRM does, and it requires almost no human input.

D1
SMART RETRY WINDOW
DAYS 0–7

Retry failed card on days 1, 3, 5, 7 with exponential back-off. Time retries to paydays and business hours — not at 3am UTC when the bank's fraud system is most aggressive.

D2
DUNNING EMAILS
DAYS 1, 4, 8, 14

Branded, human-sounding emails to the billing contact. Not panic-toned. Direct link to update payment method. CC'd to the main account contact by day 8 in case billing has moved on.

D3
AM HUMAN TOUCH
DAY 10

If automation hasn't resolved it, account manager gets a task to call. Usually a five-minute phone call fixes what ten automated emails couldn't.

D4
GRACE & SUSPEND
DAY 21–30

Grace period before any service restriction. Data preserved. Clear reactivation path. Service suspended before cancellation — cancellation is the last resort, not the first.

THE OUTCOME

CHURN STOPS BEING A SURPRISE

Problems surface in week 1, not month 11. Involuntary churn drops fast. Voluntary churn becomes a managed conversation rather than an emergency.

In a 400-customer base with average retention economics, every full percentage point of annual churn reduction is worth materially more than a new-customer acquisition campaign — and it's delivered almost entirely by the CRM.
